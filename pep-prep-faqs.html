General HIV/AIDS FAQs

What is HIV?

Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) attacks the body's immune system, specifically the CD4 cells (T cells). If untreated, HIV can lead to acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS).

What is AIDS?

Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is the final stage of HIV infection. Not everyone who has HIV will develop AIDS. With proper treatment, people with HIV can live long, healthy lives.

How is HIV transmitted?

HIV is transmitted through contact with certain body fluids such as blood, semen, vaginal fluids, pre-seminal fluids, rectal fluids, and breast milk. The most common ways it is spread include unprotected sex, sharing needles, and from mother to child during childbirth or breastfeeding.

What are the symptoms of HIV?

Symptoms of HIV can vary, but early symptoms may resemble flu-like illnesses, including fever, sore throat, and rash. As the virus progresses without treatment, it weakens the immune system, which can lead to more serious infections and illnesses.

What is the difference between HIV and AIDS?

HIV is the virus that causes AIDS. HIV damages the immune system, while AIDS is a condition where the immune system becomes severely damaged, leading to opportunistic infections or cancers.

PEP & PrEP FAQs

What is PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis)?

PEP is a short-term treatment used to prevent HIV infection after potential exposure. It involves taking HIV medicines within 72 hours after a possible exposure to HIV, such as unprotected sex or needle sharing.

How does PEP work?

PEP works by preventing the virus from multiplying in the body. It must be started within 72 hours after exposure and is taken for 28 days. It is not a guaranteed prevention method, but it greatly reduces the risk of HIV infection.

Who should take PEP?

PEP is recommended for individuals who may have been exposed to HIV in situations such as unprotected sex, sexual assault, or sharing needles. It is important to seek medical advice as soon as possible to determine if PEP is appropriate.

What is PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis)?

PrEP is a medication taken by HIV-negative individuals to reduce their risk of getting HIV. It involves taking a daily pill that is highly effective at preventing HIV when taken consistently.

How does PrEP work?

PrEP works by blocking HIV from establishing an infection in the body. When taken correctly, PrEP has been shown to reduce the risk of HIV infection by more than 90% in people who are at high risk.

Who should take PrEP?

PrEP is recommended for people at high risk of HIV, such as those with HIV-positive partners, individuals who engage in unprotected sex, or those who inject drugs and share needles.

Are PEP and PrEP the same thing?

No, PEP is taken after a potential HIV exposure to prevent infection, while PrEP is taken before any exposure to HIV to reduce the risk of infection.
