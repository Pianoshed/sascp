Understanding PEP and PrEP

  • PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis): PEP is an emergency treatment for those who may have been exposed to HIV. To be effective, PEP must be started within 72 hours after potential exposure and involves taking antiretroviral medicines for 28 days. It is available for individuals who think they may have been exposed to HIV through sexual contact, needle-sharing, or other potential risks.
  • PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis): PrEP is a preventive treatment for people who are at high risk of HIV infection. It involves taking a daily pill to reduce the chance of contracting HIV before any exposure occurs. When taken consistently, PrEP has been shown to reduce the risk of HIV transmission by up to 99%.

Why PEP and PrEP Are Important

  • PEP: PEP provides an emergency response to potential exposure and can significantly reduce the risk of HIV infection if started quickly. It is a crucial intervention for preventing HIV after unexpected or accidental exposure.
  • PrEP: For those at ongoing risk of exposure, PrEP offers an added layer of protection that reduces anxiety and helps individuals manage their sexual health proactively. PrEP is especially important for those in high-risk groups, including those with HIV-positive partners.
